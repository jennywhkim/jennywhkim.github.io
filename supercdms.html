As part of my summer work for SuperCDMS (Cryogenic Dark Matter Search) in the Figueroa Lab, I worked on PCB and electronics fixture design for the cryogenic equipment in the new testing facility at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory. I designed PCBs for the cold electronics stages (4K and below). Aside from these projects, I also worked on magnetic field analysis for the equipment using COMSOL Multiphysics.


Background

The team at Northwestern for the research project (SuperCDMS) I was working with was in the process of building a completely new underground facility at Fermilab for dark matter detection. SuperCDMS takes a TES (transition-edge sensor) approach to the dark matter search â€“ when the TES/detector reaches 45 mK, it is superconducting and can detect any small change in temperature, which is ideal for detecting dark matter. The equipment is composed of cans, arranged like a Russian doll, where each layer, or temperature stage, inside is colder. To detect such a small amount of energy at such low temperatures, the PCBs had to minimize parasitic resistances and/or be superconducting.
